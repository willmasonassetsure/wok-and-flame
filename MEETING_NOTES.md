# Wok & Flame — Client Walkthrough

Prep notes for the demo meeting. Not a script — talking points. The goal is to walk him through what's built, why each piece earns its place, and land the bigger "site vs no-site" argument without lecturing.

---

## 0. Frame the meeting (2 min, before you open the site)

**Open with the business case, not the site.**

Say something like:

> "Before I show you the site — quick honest question. How much business do you think comes from people who already know you vs people who don't? Because the site is really only about the second group."

Listen. Then drop the frame:

- Just Eat is a **marketplace** — he's one tile in a grid next to every other Chinese takeaway in South Manchester.
- A site is a **shopfront** — the only place on the internet where Wok & Flame is the only name on the screen.
- People Googling *"best Chinese West Didsbury"* or *"Chinese takeaway M20"* don't land on Just Eat. They land on websites, Google Maps, and Google Business Profiles. **No site = not in that conversation.**
- He's already done the hardest part — **11,375 Just Eat reviews, 5.0 stars**. That's a moat. This site's job is to put that moat in front of every new visitor before they even think about comparing.

**Takeaway line to land early:**
> "This isn't a replacement for Just Eat. It's the thing that makes new customers pick you on Just Eat."

---

## 1. The story angle — weaponise what he already has

Three things about Wok & Flame that nobody else on Burton Road can copy. The whole site is built around these:

| Asset | Where it shows up |
|---|---|
| **11,375 Just Eat + 256 Google reviews** | Hero, About stats, Reviews section header |
| **Real wok cooking, cooked to order** | Brand identity (fire, embers, red), tagline, About copy |
| **Chinese-primary, Didsbury-specific** | Positioning everywhere — not "pan-Asian", not "fusion", not vague |

**The rule we stuck to:** every claim on the site is grounded in something real — a review count, a dish that's actually on the menu, a postcode. No invented "for the regulars who ask" filler. Specifics beat fluff in both SEO and trust.

## 2. Feature-by-feature walkthrough

Open the site on a phone *and* a laptop side by side if possible. Most of the work is mobile-first.

### Loading screen
- 2.5 seconds of embers rising + "WOK / FLAME" wordmark unveiling letter-by-letter.
- **What to say**: "First impression. Cheap sites show a boring spinner. Yours sets the temperature of the brand before they see a single word."
- **Subtext to land**: this level of polish signals "real business", not "built on a Squarespace template at midnight".

### Hero
- Review count front and centre: **11,375 Just Eat reviews**.
- Chinese-primary tagline, two CTAs: Order on Just Eat + Find Us.
- **What to say**: "Three-second decision. Either they trust you enough to tap Order, or they leave. That review count does most of the selling — we just put it where their eyes go first."

### About
- Plain-English story: proper Chinese takeaway, West Didsbury, cooked to order, traditional wok.
- Three stats: **5.0 rating**, **11,375 reviews**, **M20**.
- Named dishes: salt and pepper, aromatic duck, chow mein, sweet & sour. Shorter Thai section: pad Thai, tom yum, green/red curries.
- **What to say**: "Nobody trusts brand voice on a takeaway site. They trust numbers and specifics. This section gives them both — no filler."

### Menu (biggest feature — spend time here)

**Desktop walkthrough:**
- 15 categories, ordered the way UK Chinese customers actually browse: Set Meals (value opener) → Specials → starters → the British hero, Salt & Pepper → proteins → carbs → extras → desserts → drinks.
- Floating wok icon "tethered" to whichever category is active — a brand detail, not decoration.
- Popular Picks strip at the top — cross-category bestsellers one tap away.
- Legend at the bottom: chilli / popular / veg — only bright when the category contains them.

**Mobile walkthrough (this is where 90%+ of orders happen):**
- Category title slider — shows current category, icons scroll horizontally, **auto-centres when you tap** a new category.
- **Jump To grid** — 3 cols × 5 rows of every category. The primary mobile nav. One tap to anything.
- Indicator row — tells you at a glance if this category has spicy, popular, or veg options. Brightness reflects reality (audited against the menu data item by item).
- Dish rows: Popular tag, chilli dot, description, price.

**What to say:**
> "Just Eat's menu is functional but generic. Yours is faster to scan on a phone, tells customers what's in each section before they scroll, and has personality. The menu is in code — change a price in one place and the whole site updates."

### Reviews
- Headline: **"What our regulars say"** — grounded, not hyperbolic.
- Two stat pills: **11,375 Just Eat** + **256 Google**, both linking out.
- **What to say**: "Social proof isn't a testimonial card. It's a real number next to a platform logo they already trust."

### Gallery
- Masonry grid — wok cooking, dumplings, interior, salt and pepper chicken.
- **What to say**: "Restaurants live on food photography. Just Eat gives you one tile photo. Your site gives you a full gallery. These are stock for now — send me real kitchen/food shots and they swap in."

### Find Us / Footer
- Address (Burton Road, M20 2LW), Just Eat link, socials, navigation.
- **What to say**: "Google reads your address and opening hours off this page for 'Chinese takeaway near me' results. It's quiet SEO work the site does automatically."

## 3. Site vs no-site — the argument in plain English

If he pushes back with "but I've got Just Eat, do I need this?" — here's the lineup:

| Without a site | With this site |
|---|---|
| One tile next to 20 competitors | Your own shopfront |
| Just Eat decides your photo | You decide every pixel |
| New customers find you by accident | You show up in Google results |
| Nobody sees the 11k+ reviews until they're already on JE | Reviews are the first thing every new visitor sees |
| Your brand is what Just Eat's template allows | Your brand is wok fire, embers, confidence |
| Menu changes require Just Eat admin | Menu is in code — updated in minutes |
| No direct relationship with customers | Email, socials, eventually direct orders |
| Paying ~30% JE commission on every regular | Route repeat customers direct, keep the margin |

**Power line:**
> "Every new regular you earn through Just Eat costs you about 30% for life. Every new regular who finds you via Google and orders direct costs you nothing after acquisition. That maths is why a site pays for itself."

---

## 4. What we need from him to finish this off

Frame these as quick asks, not homework:

- **Real photos** — kitchen shots, 5–10 signature dishes plated, a shot of him. Stock goes, his stuff goes in.
- **Social handles** — IG / TikTok / FB. Footer icons are placeholders.
- **Phone number** — for collection orders (not on the site yet, deliberate — wanted his call on whether he wants inbound calls).
- **Allergen info** — do you have a PDF / doc we can link to?
- **Anything he wants to promote** — seasonal dishes, events, loyalty offers?

---

## 5. Upsells (plant seeds, don't pitch)

Only raise if he asks "what else could we do?" — or naturally, if the conversation's going well:

- **Google Business Profile** optimisation — same review engine, different surface. Tied to the site.
- **Monthly content** — seasonal specials, new dishes, kitchen stories on the site.
- **Direct-order system** — cut Just Eat's cut on repeat customers who now find you direct.
- **Email capture + monthly specials newsletter** — builds an audience he owns.
- **Instagram content plan** — tied to the brand direction we just built (fire, wok, red, confidence).

---

## 6. Closing

Land this line at the end:

> "Just Eat is the marketplace — keep it, it's doing its job. This is your **shopfront**. It's where new customers find you, where your 11,000+ reviews do the work they were earned to do, and where your brand lives independently of any platform."

Then shut up and let him respond.
